H. H. Asquith (1910-12-19 to 1916-12-06)
End Reason: Resigned due to loss of support within his wartime coalition cabinet.
Notes: Led a Liberal minority government supported by Irish Nationalists and Labour, forming a wartime coalition in 1915 before being replaced by David Lloyd George.

David Lloyd George (1916-12-06 to 1918-12-14)
End Reason: Parliament dissolved for the 1918 general election following the Armistice.
Notes: Formed a wartime coalition government relying heavily on Conservative support, despite having no personal election mandate.

David Lloyd George (1918-12-14 to 1922-10-19)
End Reason: Resigned after the Conservative Party voted to withdraw from the coalition at the Carlton Club meeting.
Notes: Led the 'Coupon Coalition' to a landslide victory. Handled the aftermath of WWI and the Anglo-Irish Treaty.

Bonar Law (1922-10-23 to 1922-11-15)
End Reason: Parliament dissolved to seek an explicit mandate in the 1922 general election.
Notes: Formed a Conservative government after the collapse of the Lloyd George coalition.

Bonar Law (1922-11-15 to 1923-05-20)
End Reason: Resigned due to terminal throat cancer.
Notes: Known as the 'Unknown Prime Minister', his term was cut short by severe illness.

Stanley Baldwin (1923-05-22 to 1923-12-06)
End Reason: Called an early general election to seek a mandate for protectionist trade tariffs.
Notes: Inherited Bonar Law's majority but quickly sought his own mandate.

Stanley Baldwin (1923-12-06 to 1924-01-22)
End Reason: Resigned after losing a vote of no confidence on the King's Speech.
Notes: Lost his majority in the 1923 election but briefly remained in office until defeated in the Commons.

Ramsay MacDonald (1924-01-22 to 1924-11-04)
End Reason: Resigned after losing a vote of no confidence over the Campbell Case, leading to the 1924 general election.
Notes: The first-ever Labour Prime Minister, heading a short-lived minority government with passive Liberal support.

Stanley Baldwin (1924-11-04 to 1929-06-04)
End Reason: Defeated in the 1929 general election, resulting in a hung parliament.
Notes: Term marked by the 1926 General Strike and the 1928 act granting equal voting rights to women.

Ramsay MacDonald (1929-06-05 to 1931-08-24)
End Reason: Resigned due to a cabinet split over proposed budget cuts during the Great Depression.
Notes: Second Labour minority government, dominated by the fallout of the 1929 Wall Street Crash.

Ramsay MacDonald (1931-08-24 to 1931-10-27)
End Reason: Parliament dissolved for the 1931 general election to secure a mandate for the National Government.
Notes: Formed the first National Government emergency coalition; subsequently expelled from the Labour Party.

Ramsay MacDonald (1931-10-27 to 1935-06-07)
End Reason: Resigned due to declining physical and mental health.
Notes: Led the National Government after a landslide election victory, though the coalition was dominated numerically by Conservatives.

Stanley Baldwin (1935-06-07 to 1935-11-14)
End Reason: Parliament dissolved for the 1935 general election.
Notes: Succeeded MacDonald as head of the National Government shortly before the general election.

Stanley Baldwin (1935-11-14 to 1937-05-28)
End Reason: Retired voluntarily after the coronation of King George VI.
Notes: Navigated the 1936 Abdication Crisis of King Edward VIII and the early stages of rearmament.

Neville Chamberlain (1937-05-28 to 1940-05-10)
End Reason: Resigned following the Norway Debate and a loss of cross-party support during WWII.
Notes: Associated with the appeasement of Nazi Germany (Munich Agreement) before leading the UK into WWII.

Winston Churchill (1940-05-10 to 1945-07-26)
End Reason: Defeated in the 1945 general election following the end of the war in Europe.
Notes: Led the all-party wartime coalition government to victory in WWII.

Clement Attlee (1945-07-26 to 1950-02-23)
End Reason: Parliament dissolved for the 1950 general election.
Notes: Landslide victory for Labour; introduced the National Health Service (NHS) and the modern welfare state.

Clement Attlee (1950-02-23 to 1951-10-26)
End Reason: Defeated in the 1951 general election despite winning the popular vote.
Notes: Governed with a very narrow majority; called an early election to try and increase it.

Winston Churchill (1951-10-26 to 1955-04-05)
End Reason: Resigned due to declining health and old age.
Notes: His final term as Prime Minister, marking the transition into the post-war consensus and the coronation of Elizabeth II.

Anthony Eden (1955-04-06 to 1955-05-26)
End Reason: Parliament dissolved for the 1955 general election.
Notes: Succeeded Churchill and immediately called an election to secure his own mandate.

Anthony Eden (1955-05-26 to 1957-01-09)
End Reason: Resigned due to ill health in the aftermath of the Suez Crisis.
Notes: His premiership was derailed by the diplomatic and political failure of the 1956 Suez Crisis.

Harold Macmillan (1957-01-10 to 1959-10-08)
End Reason: Parliament dissolved for the 1959 general election.
Notes: Restored party morale after Suez, overseeing a period of rising affluence.

Harold Macmillan (1959-10-08 to 1963-10-18)
End Reason: Resigned due to ill health amid the political fallout of the Profumo affair.
Notes: Delivered the 'Wind of Change' speech regarding decolonization; late term overshadowed by the Profumo sex-and-spying scandal.

Alec Douglas-Home (1963-10-19 to 1964-10-16)
End Reason: Defeated in the 1964 general election.
Notes: Renounced his peerage (Earl of Home) to sit in the House of Commons as Prime Minister.

Harold Wilson (1964-10-16 to 1966-03-31)
End Reason: Called an early general election to increase his razor-thin parliamentary majority.
Notes: First Wilson administration; governed effectively with a working majority of just four seats.

Harold Wilson (1966-03-31 to 1970-06-19)
End Reason: Defeated in the 1970 general election.
Notes: Oversaw major liberalizing social reforms, including the decriminalization of homosexuality and abolition of capital punishment.

Edward Heath (1970-06-19 to 1974-03-04)
End Reason: Resigned after failing to form a coalition following a hung parliament in the February 1974 election.
Notes: Led the UK into the European Economic Community (EEC); term plagued by industrial unrest and the Three-Day Week.

Harold Wilson (1974-03-04 to 1974-10-10)
End Reason: Called an early general election to secure a working majority.
Notes: Formed a minority government after the February 1974 hung parliament.

Harold Wilson (1974-10-10 to 1976-04-05)
End Reason: Resigned unexpectedly, citing a long-held plan to retire at age 60.
Notes: Held the 1975 referendum which confirmed UK membership in the EEC. Succeeded by James Callaghan.

James Callaghan (1976-04-05 to 1979-05-04)
End Reason: Defeated in the 1979 general election after losing a vote of no confidence.
Notes: Term overshadowed by severe industrial strikes known as the 'Winter of Discontent' (1978-79).

Margaret Thatcher (1979-05-04 to 1983-06-09)
End Reason: Parliament dissolved for the 1983 general election.
Notes: The UK's first female Prime Minister. First term defined by monetarist policies and victory in the Falklands War.

Margaret Thatcher (1983-06-09 to 1987-06-11)
End Reason: Parliament dissolved for the 1987 general election.
Notes: Defeated the 1984-85 miners' strike and survived the Brighton hotel bombing (1984).

Margaret Thatcher (1987-06-11 to 1990-11-28)
End Reason: Resigned after a leadership challenge and a loss of cabinet support.
Notes: Longest-serving 20th-century PM. Her downfall was driven by the unpopular Community Charge ('Poll Tax') and divisions over Europe.

John Major (1990-11-28 to 1992-04-09)
End Reason: Parliament dissolved for the 1992 general election.
Notes: Succeeded Thatcher mid-term and led the UK through the Gulf War.

John Major (1992-04-09 to 1997-05-02)
End Reason: Defeated in the 1997 general election.
Notes: Faced 'Black Wednesday' (1992), deep Conservative divisions over the Maastricht Treaty, and numerous sleaze scandals.

Tony Blair (1997-05-02 to 2001-06-07)
End Reason: Parliament dissolved for the 2001 general election.
Notes: Won a landslide victory for 'New Labour'. Signed the Good Friday Agreement (1998) and introduced Scottish and Welsh devolution.

Tony Blair (2001-06-07 to 2005-05-05)
End Reason: Parliament dissolved for the 2005 general election.
Notes: Term profoundly shaped by the September 11 attacks, the War on Terror, and the highly controversial 2003 invasion of Iraq.

Tony Blair (2005-05-05 to 2007-06-27)
End Reason: Resigned voluntarily to hand power to Gordon Brown.
Notes: Led Labour to three consecutive election victories but left office amid declining popularity largely stemming from the Iraq War.

Gordon Brown (2007-06-27 to 2010-05-11)
End Reason: Resigned after the 2010 general election resulted in a hung parliament and failed coalition talks.
Notes: Term dominated by his response to the 2007-2008 global financial crisis and subsequent recession.

David Cameron (2010-05-11 to 2015-05-07)
End Reason: Parliament dissolved for the 2015 general election.
Notes: Headed the first formal UK coalition government (Conservative-Lib Dem) since WWII, implementing major fiscal austerity measures.

David Cameron (2015-05-07 to 2016-07-13)
End Reason: Resigned after losing the 2016 Brexit referendum on EU membership.
Notes: Secured an unexpected Conservative majority in 2015, but his premiership ended when the UK voted to leave the EU.

Theresa May (2016-07-13 to 2017-06-08)
End Reason: Called an early general election to strengthen her mandate for Brexit negotiations.
Notes: The UK's second female PM; succeeded Cameron uncontested after the Conservative leadership race collapsed.

Theresa May (2017-06-08 to 2019-07-24)
End Reason: Resigned after her Brexit withdrawal agreement was repeatedly rejected by Parliament.
Notes: Lost her parliamentary majority in the 2017 election, forcing a confidence-and-supply arrangement with the DUP.

Boris Johnson (2019-07-24 to 2019-12-12)
End Reason: Parliament dissolved for the 2019 general election to break the parliamentary Brexit deadlock.
Notes: Won the Conservative leadership race after May's resignation, taking a hardline stance on Brexit negotiations.

Boris Johnson (2019-12-12 to 2022-09-06)
End Reason: Resigned following mass cabinet resignations sparked by a series of ethics and conduct scandals.
Notes: Won an 80-seat majority, finalized the UK's departure from the EU, and managed the COVID-19 pandemic, but was undone by 'Partygate' and the Pincher affair.

Liz Truss (2022-09-06 to 2022-10-25)
End Reason: Resigned after her 'mini-budget' triggered severe financial market turmoil and lost her the confidence of her party.
Notes: The shortest-serving Prime Minister in UK history (49 days). Her term coincided with the death of Queen Elizabeth II.

Rishi Sunak (2022-10-25 to 2024-07-05)
End Reason: Defeated in the 2024 general election.
Notes: The first British Asian PM. Succeeded Truss mid-term; struggled with high inflation and party divisions before calling a July 2024 election.

Keir Starmer (2024-07-05 to Present)
End Reason: N/A
Notes: Led the Labour Party to a landslide victory in the 2024 general election, ending 14 years of Conservative rule.
